Arthroscopy

Arthroscopy or keyhole surgery of the knee is a very useful technique for dealing with knee problems in a less invasive manner. Two small holes are made in the knee either side of the patellar tendon, one for the camera and one for the instrument to undertake any therapeutic procedure. Knee arthroscopy is indicated for patients who have mechanical symptoms, e.g. locking or giving way. It is usual practice, having assessed the patient, to undertake further investigation with an MRI scan to determine the intra-articular problem before recommending knee arthroscopy.

Knee arthroscopy only has a limited role to play in the arthritic knee, although, if there are associated loose bodies or a tear in the meniscus, arthroscopy can be of benefit to many older patients. The meniscus is the shock absorber between the femur and the tibia and tears within this structure can lead to pain, swelling and locking and resection or repair of the tear is often necessary. If the tear is repaired, the patient will have to undertake a period of protected weight bearing through the knee.

To summarise, knee arthroscopy can be useful for a number of conditions and is the first line of surgical treatment for meniscal tears, removal of loose bodies and other causes of locking, excision of chondral flaps, biopsy and knee washouts for infection. As it is a minimally invasive procedure, recovery is normally fast and infection rates are low.

Complications of surgery

Even when surgery is undertaken to a high standard, complications can occur. Many complications of surgery are specific to a certain procedure and these will be discussed on an individual basis with patient, both in the outpatient setting before decision for surgery is made, and also during the consent process for surgery. Infection is always a concern for patients and surgeons and if artificial material or an implant is being used, prophylactic antibiotics are normally given at the time of surgery. With lower limb surgery, particularly more major surgery such as hip and knee replacement, the risk of developing blood clot or thrombosis is recognised and some form of anticoagulant is administered for a period of time to decrease this risk. Early mobilisation and an enhanced recovery helps to decrease the risk of developing deep vein thrombosis. In hip and knee replacement, other specific complications include; post-operative stiffness of the joint; leg length difference; increased risk of falling in the first few weeks following surgery which may lead to fracture or dislocation. The use of a tourniquet in lower limb surgery can rarely be associated with damage to neurovascular structures. The majority of orthopaedic surgical procedures undertaken have a very high surgical success rate and the risk of developing one of the complications listed above is very small.
